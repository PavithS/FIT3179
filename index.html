Global Immigration (1990-2019)

A visualisation exploring global immigration by country, continent, development and gender.

Where are immigrants travelling?

Asia and Europe consistently have the highest number of immigrants. Europe's high immigrant stock is possibly influenced by high migration within the continent due the freedom of movement in the EU.[1]

Due to the large population in Asia, immigrants per capita in the region remains lower than other regions; China is the lowest globally with 0.7 per capita in 2019. North America also receives a large portion of global immigration with the US containing a fifth of the global migrant population in 2013.[2]

Countries in the Middle-East lead immigrants per capita by a sizeable margin, which is sustained by their large and growing migrant worker population.[3]

The Gender Demographics of immigrants

The Middle-East has a high percentage of male immigrants, especially Oman where 83.6% of immigrants are male, due to a high intake of migrant labourers, 61% of whom are male.[4]

In contrast, migrant population in Nepal is 70% female even though the country is embedded in a region in Asia where most migrants are male. Every continent except Asia (where 58% are male) remained near a 50-50 gender balance between 1990-2019.

Female migrants have accounted for more than 50% of Europe's migrant population since 1990, however, in Europe female migrants have faced greater difficulty in finding employment than any other group, female refugees have an unemployment rate of 45%.[5]

Development and Income in destination countries

Most immigration occurs in more developed, high income countries, with many migrating for work and sending remittances back to their families, most of whom are living in middle-income countries.[6]

Immigration typically has positive impacts by increase the working age population and bringing skills that contribute to the growth of economies, 70% of the workforce increase in Europe between 2004-2014 was due to migrants.[7]
